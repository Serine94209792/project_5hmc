Multi-dimensional 5-hydroxymethylcytosine features in cell-free DNA enable detection, staging and subtyping of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma

Abstract
Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is a highly lethal malignan t cancer with limited biomarkers for early detection and disease stratification. Here, we investigated whether multi-dimensional 5-hydroxymethylcytosine (5hmC) features in plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) could support noninvasive detection, staging, and subtyping of PDAC. We performed genome-wide cfDNA 5hmC analysis in 274 individuals, including 200 patients with PDAC and 74 non-PDAC controls, and extracted seven categories of features covering both coverage-based and fragmentomic signals. PDAC was characterized by widespread and structured 5hmC alterations across multiple genomic and fragment-level feature classes, indicating broad epigenetic remodeling and ultimately pointing to multi-tissue perturbations rather than pancreatic tissue contribution alone. Stage-related analyses revealed a progressive shift from early developmental and metabolic programs toward later immune- and stromal-associated programs. Pathological subtype analysis further supported a stepwise progression pattern defined by lymph node metastasis and vascular invasion, with distinct molecular features underlying different invasive routes. Motivated by these findings , we developed a two-level machine-learning framework integrating multiple 5hmC feature types. The final stacked model achieved strong performance for PDAC detection (ROC-AUC = 0.952), while the staging model showed moderate discrimination (macro-AUC = 0.721) and the subtyping model demonstrated good performance (micro-AUC = 0.831; macro-AUC = 0.818). These findings demonstrate that multi-dimensional cfDNA 5hmC profiling provides a promising noninvasive framework for PDAC detection, stage assessment, and pathological subtyping.
Introduction
Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is one of the most lethal malignancies worldwide, with a 5-year relative survival rate of 13 .3 % between 2015-2021 [1] . This poor prognosis largely results from occult onset and late diagnosis, as approximately 79% of patients present with locally advanced or metastatic disease at the time of diagnosis [1] , together with biological heterogeneity that complicates clinical management [2] . Current clinical tools, including imaging and serum biomarkers such as carbohydrate antigen 19-9 (CA 19-9), remain inadequate for early detection. CA 19-9 shows limited sensitivity (7 9 –8 1 %) and specificity (82-90%) , thereby reducing its clinical utility [3] . Moreover, a subset of individuals (5–10%) are Lewis antigen–negative and do not produce CA 19-9, further limiting its applicability [4] . I maging approaches exhibit reduced sensitivity for small or early-stage tumors ( approximately 50% ~70% for <2cm lesions) , thereby limiting reliable detection at an early stage [5] . Moreover, imaging-based approaches are not suitable for population-level screening, further limiting their utility in early diagnosis. Therefore, there is a pressing need for noninvasive biomarkers that can not only detect PDAC but also capture clinically relevant information related to tumor stage and subtype.
Liquid biopsy offers a minimally invasive approach to assess tumor-associated signals in peripheral blood, and cell-free DNA (cfDNA) has emerged as a particularly attractive analyte for PDAC detection and monitoring [6] . Previous studies have explored cfDNA mutations, methylation, fragment size profiles, and end motifs in PDAC , yet each modality captures only a limited aspect of disease biology [7] . Notably, mutation-based assays are constrained by low tumor fractions, particularly in early-stage disease, resulting in reduced sensitivity [8] . Moreover, single-modality biomarkers are insufficient to fully capture the dynamic and multi-layered biological processes underlying tumor progression and invasion [7] . Increasing evidence suggests that cfDNA contains not only tumor-derived signals but also broader host-related and tissue-remodeling information [9] , raising the possibility that integrative profiling of multiple cfDNA features may provide a more comprehensive view of disease status [10] .
5-Hydroxymethylcytosine (5hmC), an epigenetic mark associated with active gene regulation and tissue-specific transcriptional programs, has shown promise as a cfDNA-based biomarker in multiple cancers [11] . Because 5hmC is enriched in gene bodies and regulatory regions and exhibits strong tissue specificity, genome-wide 5hmC profiling may provide a biologically informative view of cancer-associated epigenetic remodeling [12, 13] . However, most prior studies have focused mainly on cancer-versus-control discrimination, and the potential of cfDNA 5hmC for PDAC staging and pathological subtyping has not been systematically explored [11] . In addition, existing studies have generally emphasized a single class of 5hmC-derived signal, while the multi-dimensional feature s of cfDNA 5hmC information, including both coverage-based and fragment-level characteristics, has not been systematically investigated in PDAC.
PDAC progression is biologically heterogeneous . Beyond conventional TNM staging, pathological features such as lymph node metastasis and vascular invasion reflect distinct dimensions of tumor aggressiveness and are closely associated with prognosis [14, 15] . These clinicopathological features likely arise from dynamic biological programs, including developmental reprogramming, stromal remodeling, immune interaction, and invasive adaptation [16, 17] . A biomarker framework capable of capturing these processes could improve not only diagnosis but also biological stratification of PDAC. We therefore hypothesized that integrating multiple classes of cfDNA 5hmC features would better characterize the epigenetic landscape of PDAC and provide complementary information for detection, stage prediction, and subtype inference.
In this study, we performed genome-wide 5hmC-Seal profiling of plasma cfDNA from patients with PDAC and non-PDAC controls and systematically extracted seven categories of 5hmC-derived features , including gene-level signals, genomic windows, consensus peaks, pancreatic cancer-associated open chromatin regions (OCR) , fragment size distribution (FSD) , repeat-associated features, and 4-mer end motifs . We first characterized the genomic distribution of cfDNA 5hmC signals in PDAC, and then investigated how these features varied with disease status, pathological stage, and invasion-related subtype progression. Finally, we developed a two-level machine-learning framework to evaluate the clinical utility of multi-dimensional cfDNA 5hmC features for PDAC detection, staging, and subtyping. Through this integrative approach, we aimed to define a more comprehensive noninvasive epigenetic framework for PDAC characterization and to provide insight into the biological programs underlying tumor progression and pathological heterogeneity.
Results
study design and clinicopathological characteristics of participants
A total of 274 adult patients (>=18 years) were enrolled from Peking Union Medical College Hospital between January 2024 and December 202 4 , including 20 4 patients with pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) and 7 0 non-PDAC individuals ( Fig. 1A , Supplementary Table 1 ) . All diagnoses were confirmed by postoperative patholog y and all peripheral blood samples were collected before any treatment (surgery, chemotherapy or radiotherapy) . T he baseline clinical and pathological data w as shown in Supplementary Table 1 . Following the standard protocols, p lasma cfDNA was then isolated from blood samples for library construction, then 5hmC-Seal profiling and low-input whole-genome 5hmC sequencing ( Fig. 1B ) . Finally, genome-wide 5hmC data was generated for downstream analysis . To comprehensively capture the genome-wide features of 5hmC-marked cfDNA , 7 categories of 5hmC features were extracted, including coverage-based signals across genes, genomic fixed windows, 5hmC signal peaks, and pancreatic cancer specific open chromatin regions (OCR) , as well as fragmentomic features such as fragment size distribution (FSD) , genomic repeat elements (Artemis) , and 4-mer end motifs ( Fig. 1C ) . To investigate the roles of 5hmC in different clinical characteristics, differential feature analysis was performed to identify PDAC-associated signals, stage-related alterations, and subtype-specific differentiation patterns ( Fig. 1D ). Finally, a multilevel modeling framework was estab lished using all 5hmC feature types to diagnose PDAC patients, further predict clinicopathological stage and distinguish differentiation subtypes ( Fig. 1E ) .
Genomic distribution of 5hm C in cfDNA
The distribution of 5hmC signals across the genome was not random. A fter quality control and read alignment, 5hmC signals across different genomic functional regions were quantified and normalized, and the distribution of 5hmC signals among these regions was compared. Overall, 5hmC signals were mainly enriched in gene bodies, with a strong preference for intronic regions, where the signal intensity was about ten times higher than in exon s . Among untranslated regions, the 3′UTR exhibited markedly higher 5hmC levels than the 5′UTR, while CpG islands displayed stronger enrichment than enhancers ( Fig. 2C ) . Metagene profiling further showed a characteristic depletion of 5hmC around the transcription start site (TSS) and transcription end site (TES), with relatively elevated signal across gene-flanking and gene-body regions ( Fig. 2B ) . In addition, tissue-specific enhancer profiling demonstrated dynamic variation of 5hmC signal across the regions, supporting the preferential localization of cfDNA 5hmC within defined gene regulatory elements ( Fig. 2A ) . In summary, these results indicate that 5hmC in cfDNA is distributed in a highly structured, with preferential enrichment in gene-associated and regulatory regions rather than a random genome-wide pattern .
Distinct 5hmC signatures distinguish PDAC from non-PDAC
To characterize PDAC-associated alterations in cfDNA hydroxymethylation , we systematically compared 5hmC-derived features between patients with PDAC and non-PDAC controls across multiple feature classes, including genomic windows, gene-level signals, open chromatin regions, peaks, fragment size distribution, end motifs, and repeat-associated elements . Differential features were detected across the genome and were not confined to a single category, indicating widespread remodeling of the 5hmC landscape in PDAC. Genome-wide comparison of coverage-derived 5hmC features between PDAC and non-PDAC samples revealed that the m ajority of differential features are up-regulated in PDAC patients , with differential windows showing the highest number, while differential peaks showed the lowest number. ( Fig. 3A , Supplementary F ig . 1 B ) . Using these differential features for principal component analysis, the first two principal components with the largest variance clearly separated PDAC from non-PDAC patients ( Supplementary Fig. 1 A ) . Differential analysis of 5′-end 4-mer motifs showed that PDAC patients were enriched for motifs starting with T at the 5′ position, while motifs starting with G were decreased, whereas the opposite pattern was observed in non-PDAC samples ( Fig. 3B ) . Compared with cfDNA whole genome sequencing (WGS) end-motif analysis, tumors showed an increased proportion of motifs starting with T and a decreased proportion of motifs starting with C [18] . Consistent with previous WGS studies , the motif diversity score (MDS) calculated based on 5hmC-seal end-motif analysis was also markedly increased in PDAC patients ( S upplementary Fig . 1 C ) . Differences in fragment length distribution between PDAC and non-PDAC patients were primarily concentrated in the mono-nucleosome peak at 160–170 bp and the flanking intermediate-length regions (140–160 bp and 170–190 bp), with PDAC patients showing increased fragment abundance in these ranges ( Fig. 3C, Supplementary Fig. 1 D ) . This observation differs from previous cfDNA WGS studies, where tumor patients were reported to have a higher proportion of short fragments (<150 bp) compared with non-cancer individuals [19] . In addition, a decrease in long fragments or multi- nucleosomal fragments (>220 bp) was observed in PDAC patients, but the difference was not statistically significant ( Supplementary Fig. 1 D ) . Analysis of genomic repetitive elements showed that the largest number of significant differences was observed in the long interspersed nuclear element (LINE) family, mainly characterized by increased k- mer counts in PDAC. Differential satellite elements all showed increased k- mer counts in PDAC, whereas short interspersed nuclear elements (SINE) showed decreased counts in PDAC ( Fig. 3D ) .
Next, to determine the tissue origins of plasma cfDNA in patients, we performed deconvolution analysis using 5hmC peaks from different normal tissue s [13] . Tissue deconvolution analysis revealed significant shifts in the cfDNA tissue-of-origin landscape between patients with PDAC and non-PDAC controls, with altered contributions observed across multiple tissues, including central neural system tissues ( brain, hypothalamus ) , bone marrow (peripheral blood , but not significant ), breast, colon, heart, liver, skin and stomach indicating that PDAC is associated with a broad systemic redistribution of cfDNA sources rather than a change confined to a single tissue ( Fig. 3E ) . To further identify which genomic regions contribute to these differences in tissue origin , we performed tissue-specific enrichment analysis (TSEA) ( Supplementary Fig. 2A ) . R esults showed that distinct feature types captured complementary source signals: differential gene- and window-based features were predominantly enriched in neural system - related tissues . In addition, compared with other feature types, window features were more broadly distributed across various tissue types, including the digestive system (e.g., esophagus), breast, and circulatory system. D ifferential OCR features showed the strongest enrichment in cardiac-associated tissue, and differential peak features were mainly enriched in whole blood. Notably, no significant differences were observed in the pancreatic tissue contribution to cfDNA between PDAC and non-PDAC patients, and no differential feature enrichment was detected in pancreatic tissue , suggesting that the PDAC-associated 5hmC signature in plasma cfDNA is primarily driven by widespread host-related and multi-tissue epigenetic perturbations, rather than tumor-specific contributions alone. Together, these two analyses provide convergent evidence that the discriminatory 5hmC landscape in PDAC arises from coordinated, multi-organ alterations in cfDNA composition and tissue-associated hydroxymethylation patterns.
Differential coverage-derived features exhibited both shared and distinct patterns in genomic distribution and functional annotation. ChromHMM -based annotation revealed that d ifferential peak features were predominantly enriched in enhancer-associated chromatin states, particularly EnhG , with additional but weaker enrichment in active transcription start site and transcribed regions, indicating that peak-level alterations preferentially occur in distal regulatory elements ( Supplementary Fig. 2B ) . In contrast, differential window and OCR features displayed highly concordant enrichment patterns and were both strongly concentrated in the transcript ion flanking state, with OCR features showing the most pronounced enrichment, suggesting that these two feature types mainly capture 5hmC alterations in transcription-associated flanking regulatory regions. Consistent with these observations, Fisher enrichment analysis demonstrated substantial overlap among differential peak, window, and OCR features, whereas tissue-specific promoter s (TS promoter) showed minimal overlap with the differential feature sets ( Supplementary Fig. 2B ) . Notably, differential peak features showed the strongest correspondence with tissue-specific enhancers. T hese findings indicate that PDAC-associated 5hmC alterations are preferentially localized to enhancer and transcription-flanking regulatory regions, and that different feature types capture complementary but partially overlapping aspects of this regulatory remodeling. We therefore next asked whether this positional convergence and divergence were also reflected at the functional level . Functional enrichment analysis further revealed both shared and feature type-specific biological programs across the four coverage-based 5hmC feature classes ( Supplementary Fig. 3 ) . Among the top five Gene Ontology Biological Process ( GO-BP ) annotations for each feature type, gene-level features were predominantly enriched in synapse- and neuron-related processes , indicating a strong association with neuronal communication and vesicle trafficking. In contrast, OCR features were mainly enriched in developmental and prostaglandin-related processes, suggesting a regulatory profile linked to developmental patterning and inflammatory lipid signaling. Peak features showed preferential enrichment in hematopoietic and immune process . T he enrichment results of w indow features, by comparison, indicat ed broader involvement in organ development and systemic metabolic regulation. To further compare the functional relationships among feature types, all GO-BP clusters were subjected to semantic similarity reduction, and the 25 most semantically unique representative terms were selected for integrated visualization ( Fig. 3F ) . This analysis showed that gene-level features retained the broadest and most distinctive functional spectrum, whereas window features occupied an intermediate position by sharing multiple terms with both gene and peak features while also preserving distinct metabolic and developmental programs. Peak features contributed a more focused set of response-associated terms, whereas OCR features were relatively restricted to immune-regulatory and prostanoid -related processes. Together, these results indicate that the four coverage-based feature types capture partially overlapping but clearly non-redundant functional dimensions of PDAC-associated 5hmC alteration.
In conclusion, PDAC is characterized by widespread and multi-layered alterations in cfDNA 5hmC signatures, spanning coverage-based, fragmentomic , and repeat-associated features. Together, these changes distinguish PDAC from non-PDAC and indicate that the discriminatory 5hmC landscape reflects coordinated epigenetic perturbations across multiple tissues and regulatory regions .
Dynamic 5hmC changes across PDAC stages
W e first explored whether the PDAC-associated features identified from the PDAC versus non-PDAC comparison already exhibited stage-dependent behavior within the PDAC cohort. Notably, the end-motif MDS showed a modest but significant positive association with tumor stage, indicating progressive remodeling of fragment-end sequence complexity during disease advancement ( Supplementary Fig. 4 A ) . In parallel, both the concordance score and GSVA score of FSD features also increased with stage, whereas the corresponding metrics derived from other feature types showed no significant stage-dependent trend ( Supplementary Fig. 4 B) . These findings indicate that, among the PDAC-discriminative 5hmC feature types , fragmentomic signals, particularly end-motif composition and fragment size distribution, are more sensitive to intratumoral progression but still limited . This suggests that features distinguishing PDAC from non-PDAC do not fully explain the molecular variation underlying stage transition, thereby necessitating dedicated analytical strategies to systematically identify 5hmC alterations specifically associated with PDAC stage progression. We therefore screened features within the PDAC cohort using mutual information against ordinal stage (I, II, and III/IV), retained stage-informative variables under predefined thresholds, and applied non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) to obtain interpretable components whose sample-wise activation scores could be compared across stages. Across these NMF components, s ome components rose toward stages III/IV ( for instance, peak_C2, OCR_C3 ), whereas others moved in the opposite direction (peak_C 3/C1 , OCR_C1) ( Fig. 4A ). To connect component-level activity to the original features, we summarized, for each NMF basis vector, how many of the strongest-loading input features were assigned to each 5hmC modality after ranking features by their contribution to that component ( Fig. 4C ). These components vary not only in size but also in their feature composition, with distinct features contributing to different components even within the same feature type. O CR and peak derived components contained the largest numbers of top-ranked features . Among the peak-linked components, a subset of features was shared across components, which is consistent with related peak programs being distributed across more than one factor rather than collapsing into a single component. To functionally interpret stage-associated components, we performed GO enrichment using the top-loading features of each program ( Fig. 4B , Supplementary Fig. 4C ). These enrichment patterns were partly shared across programs but also retained clear program-specific features. The three peak programs showed substantial overlap in enriched GO terms, indicating that they captured a common functional backbone rather than entirely separate biological processes. By contrast, OCR_C1 shared very few terms with the other programs, consistent with a more restricted and distinct functional profile. OCR_C3 occupied an intermediate position, overlapping with the peak-derived programs while still preserving its own set of annotations. When the GO terms were further partitioned into shared and unique fractions, most peak-associated programs were dominated by shared terms, but each still retained a subset of unique annotations, indicating that these components represent related yet non-redundant functional modules ( Supplementary Fig. 4C ) . Inspection of the top enriched GO terms further clarified the biological distinctions among these programs ( Fig. 4D ) . Among the peak-derived programs, peak_C1 was closer to developmental and signaling processes, including non-canonical Wnt regulation and mesangial or smooth-muscle-like differentiation, and its decline across stages suggests that these early developmental features become less prominent as tumors progress. peak_C2, in contrast, increased with stage and shifted toward immune effector functions, with enrichment for cell-killing and Fc receptor-related terms, pointing to a stronger host-response component in more advanced disease. P eak_C3 looked different again, with stronger representation of signaling and trafficking-related functions such as phosphoinositide kinase activity, Rab protein regulation, and endopeptidase activator activity; its stage-associated decrease suggests attenuation of this regulatory program during tumor progression. The OCR-derived programs were more restricted. OCR_C1 was dominated by insulin-response and hydrolase-related terms and decreased with stage, indicating loss of metabolic response-related features during progression, whereas OCR_C3 increased toward later stages and was enriched for heat-response and vascular-associated processes, consistent with increasing stromal adaptation and microenvironmental remodeling in advanced PDAC. Taken together, these results suggest that PDAC progression is accompanied by a shift from earlier developmental and metabolic 5hmC programs toward later immune- and stromal-associated programs, with shared but non-redundant functional structure across components.
LM/VI-defined pathological subtypes reveal a stepwise differentiation trajectory in PDAC
To characterize the pathological heterogeneity of PDAC, we stratified patients into four subtypes according to lymph node metastasis (LM) and vascular invasion (VI) status, and observed a stepwise progression pattern from the non-invasive state to the dual-invasive state. We designated s 00 as the starting state and s 11 as the terminal state because these two groups represent the two extremes of pathological aggressiveness defined by LM and VI status: s 00 is double-negative for both lymph node metastasis and vascular invasion, whereas s 11 is double-positive for both features. This pathological ordering was further supported by the diffusion maps ( Supplementary Fig. 5 A ) . In both the gene- and peak-based analyses, s11 showed the clearest displacement away from s00 and occupied a more distal position along the pseudotime gradient, whereas s01 and s10 remained much closer to the s00 cluster and exhibited greater overlap with the other subtypes. Notably, pairwise comparisons identified significant pseudotime differences between s00 and s11, as well as between s11 and the single-positive states, indicating that s11 lies beyond both s01 and s10 along the inferred trajectory. By contrast, although s01 and s10 each capture one dimension of invasion, neither subtype consistently forms the most distal state across data modalities; instead, both occupy intermediate positions and retain partial proximity to s00. Based on these observations, we restricted downstream trajectory analyses to four data types, including gene, peaks, OCRs, and windows, all of which preserved the stepwise pseudotime structure across the four pathological subtypes. In contrast, end motif, FSD, and Artemis features were excluded from this part of the analysis because they did not yield significant trajectory features ( adj.P <0.05) associated with pseudotime , indicating limited ability to resolve the dynamic molecular changes accompanying progression from s00 to s11. Across the four retained data modalities, the diffusion structure further suggested that s01 and s10 may represent intermediate states between s00 and s11 ( Fig. 5A ) . In the four type s, the subtype centroids were arranged in an ordered manner, with s00 occupying the earliest region of the manifold, s11 consistently shifted toward a more advanced pseudotime position, and s01/s10 located between these two extremes. Notably, s01 and s10 were positioned closer to s00 than to s11, yet were displaced from the s00 core toward the direction of the terminal dual-positive state, supporting their interpretation as transitional rather than terminal subtypes. Taken together, these findings suggest a possible stepwise differentiation pattern in which s01 and s10 occupy intermediate positions between s00 and s11 .
On the basis of the aforementioned results , we next treated these intermediate states as two putative progression routes: a VI-associated path (s00 -> s01 -> s11) and an LM-associated path (s00 -> s10 -> s11). We then sought to identify path-specific differential features along these two routes and to further explore the potential biological roles of these features in shaping two differentiation trajectories. Specifically, for the VI-associated route, we defined candidate features by intersecting the differential features from s00 vs s01 and s01 vs s11, whereas for the LM-associated route, candidate features were obtained from the overlap between s00 vs s10 and s10 vs s11. We further assessed the correlation of these overlapping features with pseudotime using Spearman analysis, and features with adjusted P values < 0.05 were selected as the final trajectory-associated differential features. Using this criterion, the LM path yielded more trajectory-associated features than the VI path across all four feature types ( Fig. 5B ) . In parallel, the overlap between the two paths remained limited, indicating that the LM- and VI-associated routes are largely characterized by distinct molecular features ( Fig. 5B ) . To further examine the genomic relationship between the interval-based features from the two routes, we calculated the distance from each LM-associated interval to its nearest VI-associated interval. Window-level features showed the greatest spatial proximity, with a median nearest-neighbor distance of 612,000 bp and 40 direct overlaps, whereas OCR- and peak-level features were much more separated ( Fig. 5B ) . Given that genomic windows are defined as contiguous bins across the genome, whereas OCRs and peaks are discrete features distributed across different loci, these results indicate that the LM and VI routes show greater positional proximity at the window level but remain largely distinct in their OCR- and peak-associated regulatory elements. Functional enrichment analysis further supported this divergence between the two routes ( Fig. 5C ) . Features associated with the VI path were preferentially enriched for membrane-proximal signaling functions, suggesting that progression along this route may be more closely related to signal sensing and transduction at the cell surface. In contrast, LM-path features were enriched for processes linked to developmental reprogramming , cellular plasticity and dedifferentiation. Rather than representing a simple difference in feature composition, these patterns suggest that the two putative progression routes are accompanied by distinct biological programs: the VI route is more strongly associated with signaling and membrane-regulatory activities, whereas the LM route is characterized by broader changes related to cell state transition, migratory behavior, and invasive remodeling.
To obtain a n overall view of these route-specific programs, we next combined the trajectory-associated differential features from both the LM and VI paths and evaluated how they varied over pseudotime progression. Across all four data types, these features formed coherent dynamic blocks rather than scattered changes, indicating that the path-specific signals remained tightly linked to disease progression after integration ( Fig. 5D ) . Both increasing and decreasing patterns were evident along the trajectory, consistent with the loss of early-state programs and the emergence of late-state programs as samples progressed from s00 through the intermediate states toward s11. This structure was most continuous at the window level, whereas gene, OCR, and peak features showed clearer clustered transitions . Together, these patterns support coordinated 5hmC remodeling across four modalities during pathological progression. Based on these findings, when we divided trajectory-associated features into those that increased or decreased with pseudotime , a clearer biological picture emerged ( Supplementary Fig. 5 B ) . Features that rose from s00 toward s11 were linked mainly to signaling responsiveness, cytoskeletal reorganization, lipid remodeling, and developmental plasticity, suggesting that progression toward the dual-invasive state is accompanied by the gradual acquisition of programs that could support cell-state transition, motility, and adaptive interactions with the microenvironment. In contrast, features that declined along pseudotime were more closely related to basal regulatory and homeostatic functions . These opposite trends are consistent with a shift from the relatively non-invasive s00 state, which lacks lymphatic and vascular invasive capacity, to the s11 state, in which 5hmC remodeling appears to favor a more plastic and invasion-competent phenotype while attenuating programs associated with cellular maintenance and stable tissue organization.
Overall, these results support a stepwise 5hmC-associated progression model in PDAC, in which s00, s01/s10, and s11 define successive pathological states. Although the LM and VI routes converge on the same terminal phenotype, they are marked by distinct trajectory features and functional programs, indicating that dual-invasive progression can arise through partially different biological paths.
M ulti-dimensional 5hmC machine-learning framework enables PDAC detection, staging, and subtyping
We established a unified two-level machine-learning framework for PDAC detection, stage prediction, and subtype inference. At the first level, separate base models were trained for each individual 5hmC feature type, all following the same general design: supervised feature selection -> compact task-specific representation -> downstream predictor. Within this framework, high- dimensional 5hmC features were first reduced to the most informative subset for each task, then transformed into a task-suited representation suited to the prediction objective , and finally used for model construction. For the binary detection task (detection model) , class imbalance was first corrected by oversampling, followed by supervised feature selection using mutual information and/or Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion Lasso (HSIC-Lasso) [20] according to feature type, and downstream classification by FLAML [21] . For the multi-class stage task, supervised feature selection was followed by principal component analysis (PCA) to derive a compact low-dimensional representation, which was subsequently used for downstream classification by FLAML. For the subtyping task, HSIC-Lasso-selected features were directly fed into a fully connected neural network (FCN) regressor to model both continuous pseudotime and subtype-associated progression states. At the second level, we systematically evaluated all possible combinations of first-level feature-type models and integrated their outputs using logistic regression, thereby identifying the combination that yielded the best second-level predictive performance and using it to generate the final classification predictions ( Supplementary Fig. 6 A ; Fig. 1E ).
For the detection task, a separate first-level model was trained for each individual 5hmC feature type nested repeated stratified cross-validation strategy , with an outer loop of 3-fold cross-validation repeated 3 times and an inner loop of 5-fold cross-validation repeated 2 times. Because PDAC cases outnumbered non-PDAC controls by nearly 3:1 , SMOTE oversampling [22] was applied within each inner training split to reduce class imbalance before model training. After supervised feature selection , the processed features were entered into FLAML for automated classifier selection and hyperparameter optimization. Candidate classifiers in FLAML included LightGBM , XGBoost with limited tree depth, random forest, and extremely randomized trees, from which the optimal model was selected within each training fold. Because the non-PDAC cohort comprised biologically heterogeneous conditions, including benign tumors, inflammatory diseases, and other non-malignant disorders ( Supplementary Table 1 ), we initially trained the detection model using a PDAC-vs-non-PDAC design. However, we found that a subset of non-PDAC samples was repeatedly classified as PDAC (abnormal samples) , defined as non - PDAC samples with predicted PDAC probabilities > 0.8 across >=3 feature types ( Supplementary Table 7 ). These samples were mainly concentrated in patients with inflammatory diseases and other non-malignant disorders, rather than in those with benign tumors ( Supplementary Fig. 6 B ), suggesting that these conditions may share 5hmC patterns partially overlapping with PDAC-associated signals. We therefore introduced a second design called PDAC-vs-benign , by removing inflammatory and other non-malignant samples . Under this design, most of the first-level models and the second-level stacked model achieved better performance than those trained under the PDAC-v s -non-PDAC setting, while the estimator composition selected across nested cross-validation remained broadly similar between the two designs ( Supplementary Fig. 6 C - D ). Although the motif-based first-level model showed slightly higher mean test accuracy than the stacked model, the stacked model achieved the highest mean ROC- AUC across nested cross-validation. We therefore chose the stacked model as the final detection model, as its superior AUC indicates better overall and threshold-independent discrimination between PDAC and control samples ( Supplementary Fig. 6 E ) . For the detection model, the optimal stacked model retained five first-level inputs, including motif, peak, window, FSD, and OCR, which together gave the strongest predictive performance (mean ROC AUC = 0.952 0 , mean validation accuracy = 0.912 1 , mean test accuracy = 0.9038) ( Supplementary Table 8; Fig. 6 A ). Permutation importance analysis showed that the motif model contributed most strongly to the final prediction, whereas the other four feature types provided smaller but non-negligible gains ( Fig. 6B ) . This was consistent with the sample-level prediction pattern across first-level models, in which most samples were classified correctly by all feature types, while false-positive and false-negative cases differed modestly between models ( Fig. 6 C ). These differences suggest partial complementarity among feature types, allowing the stacked model to improve overall discrimination despite the dominant contribution of the motif model. In conclusion, our two-level machine-learning framework enabled robust PDAC detection by integrating complementary 5hmC feature types, with unequal contributions from different first-level models and motif features providing the dominant signal in the final stacked classifier.
For the stag ing task, we adopted the same nested repeated stratified cross-validation framework as in the detection model . Unlike the detection task, no sampling strategy was required because the stage groups were relatively balanced after merging stage IV with stage III owing to the limited number of stage IV cases ( Supplementary Table 1 ). The staging model was designed as a three-class classification task to distinguish stage I, stage II, and stage III/IV PDAC. After supervised feature selection, PCA was applied to derive a compact low-dimensional representation, thereby reducing the risk of overfitting caused by retaining too many selected features. The resulting features were then used for downstream classification with FLAML, including LightGBM , XGBoost with limited tree depth, L1 /L2 -regularized logistic regression, and extremely randomized trees. As in the detection task, we integrated all first-level staging models at the second level and evaluated all possible feature-type combinations. The best-performing stacked model initially included five feature types. However, because permutation importance was close to zero for artemis and motif, and their removal caused only minimal performance loss, we selected the more simper three-feature model composed of peak, OCR, and gene features as the final staging model ( Supplementary Table 8; Supplementary Fig. 6 F ) . Although the selected second-level model showed slightly higher mean test log loss than the OCR-based first-level model, it achieved the highest mean macro - AUC across all models ( Supplementary Fig. 6 G -H ) . Stage -wise ROC analysis of the secondary model showed modest discrimination across the three stage groups ( Fig. 6 A ). The model performed best for stage III/IV (AUC = 0.771 ± 0.066) (mean ± SD) , followed by stage I (AUC = 0.717 ± 0.065), whereas stage II showed the weakest discrimination (AUC = 0.676 ± 0.046), which likely reflects its intermediate biological position between early- and late-stage disease. Overall, the macro and micro AUCs were both 0.721, and none of the stage -specific AUCs exceeded 0.8, indicating that the staging model had only limited predictive ability. Permutation importance analysis of the secondary model showed unequal contributions from the three retained feature types, with OCR contributing most strongly, followed by gene-count and consensus peak features ( Fig. 6 B ). This ranking was consistent with the first-level results, in which the OCR-based model showed both the lowest log loss and the highest mean macro - AUC among the individual feature types. This pattern was also reflected in the error structure of the first-level models, where most misclassifications were off-by-one rather than off-by-two stage errors ( Fig. 6C ). OCR-based models showed the highest proportions of correct predictions and the lowest proportions of cross-stage misclassification, further supporting its stronge st contributions to the final staging classifier. In conclusion, our staging framework showed that OCR features carried the strongest predictive signal for PDAC stage, consistent with the close link between chromatin accessibility-related hydroxymethylation changes and disease progression. Although integration of multiple feature types improved overall multi-class discrimination, the staging model retained limited predictive ability and most errors were off-by-one rather than cross-stage misclassifications, indicating that adjacent stages remain difficult to separate robustly.
For the subtyping task, we used a nested repeated stratified cross-validation framework with an outer loop of 5-fold cross-validation repeated 2 times and an inner loop of 5-fold cross-validation repeated 3 times. Unlike the detection and staging tasks, the primary objective here was not direct classification, but prediction of sample pseudotime while preserving its monotonic increase across pathological states (s00, s01/s10, s11) . We therefore used FCN predictor as the first-level model, jointly optimizing two objectives: regression toward the continuous pseudotime value and ranking consistency across states, such that samples from more advanced states were assigned higher pseudotime scores. At the second level, the FCN-derived scores, serving as pseudotime -like summaries from each feature type, were integrated for multi-class classification to distinguish samples in s00, s01/s10, and s11 states. As noted above, motif, FSD, and Artemis features were not included in the subtyping model because they did not yield significant trajectory-associated features varying with pseudotime . As in the detection and staging tasks, we next evaluated all possible second-level combinations of first-level models and selected the optimal subset ( Supplementary Table 8 ). As in the detection and staging tasks, we then evaluated all possible second-level combinations of first-level models and selected the optimal subset ( Supplementary Table 8 ). Unlike the detection and staging frameworks, the two levels in the subtyping model served distinct but complementary roles: the first-level FCN models were primarily optimized to predict pseudotime while also preserving state discrimination, whereas the second-level model used these pseudotime -like outputs to further classify samples into discrete state s . For the first-level FCN models , the predicted scores showed clear positive correlations with pseudotime across all retained feature types, indicating that these models effectively captured the continuous progression structure of the samples ( Fig. 6D ). Among them, the gene- and window-based models showed the strongest associations with pseudotime , whereas OCR- and peak-based models retained moderate but still significant correlations. Notably, the window-based model showed the clearest separation among the three states , indicat ing that window-based 5hmC features preserve subtype progression structure more clearly than the other feature types and may offer the most informative first-level representation for downstream state classification ( Fig. 6D ). For the secondary multi-classification model, the integrated pseudotime -derived outputs achieved good overall discrimination among the three subtype states, with micro - AUC of 0.831 and macro - AUC of 0.818 ( Fig. 6 A ). S tate -wise evaluation showed that the model performed best in distinguishing the two terminal states, s00 and s11, with AUCs of 0.866 ± 0.046 and 0.917 ± 0.027 , respectively, whereas discrimination of the intermediate state s10/ s 01 was weaker ( 0.670 ± 0.091 ). This pattern is consistent with the biological positioning of s10/ s 01 between s00 and s11 along the inferred progression trajectory, making it less predic table than the two endpoint states. Permutation importance analysis showed unequal contributions of the two retained feature types to the secondary classifier, with the window -based features contributing most strongly and the gene-based features providing a smaller additional contribution ( Fig. 6E ). This is consistent with the clearer state separation achieved by the window-based first-level model. Collectively, these results demonstrate that our two-level framework can effectively model PDAC subtype progression by coupling pseudotime prediction with state classification and by integrating complementary progression signals across feature types.
Discussion
In this study, we systematically characterized multi-dimensional 5hmC features in plasma cfDNA from patients with PDAC and non-PDAC controls and evaluated their utility for disease detection, stage assessment, and pathological subtyping. By integrating seven categories of 5hmC-derived signals that captured both coverage-based and fragmentomic information, we found that PDAC was associated with widespread and structured alterations across multiple genomic and fragment-level feature classes. On the basis of these observations, we established a two-level machine-learning framework that achieved strong performance for PDAC detection, moderate performance for stage prediction, and good performance for subtype classification. Together, these findings support the value of multi-dimensional cfDNA 5hmC profiling as a noninvasive framework for PDAC characterization and further suggest that 5hmC signals encode not only tumor-associated information but also broader biological changes related to disease progression and invasion.
The clinical implications of our findings are that a single plasma-based cfDNA 5hmC assay could provide clinically relevant information across multiple aspects of PDAC management, including detection, biological stratification, and preoperative risk assessment. At present, pancreatic cancer surveillance is recommended mainly for selected high-risk individuals and relies largely on MRI and/or EUS . A lthough such surveillance can improve stage at diagnosis and survival, it remains resource-intensive, expertise-dependent, and not readily scalable to broader at-risk populations [23, 24] . In routine practice, CA19-9 is widely used, yet its limited sensitivity and specificity, as well as its false-negative results in Lewis antigen-negative individuals, restrict its value as a stand-alone tool for screening or early diagnosis [4] . In addition, many current liquid-biopsy approaches focus primarily on ctDNA mutation detection, which is often constrained by low tumor fraction in early-stage disease [6] . Our study therefore extends the role of blood-based biomarkers beyond simple cancer detection by showing that one cfDNA 5hmC assay may simultaneously capture diagnostic, stage-related, and invasion-related biological information. In other words, it not only indicates the presence of PDAC, but also provides insight into whether the disease exhibits biologically aggressive features that may inform further treatment planning. In the future, such a framework could be incorporated into clinical workflows in several ways. It could serve as a minimally invasive adjunct to imaging surveillance in high-risk individuals, helping identify those who may benefit from intensified MRI/EUS evaluation or shorter follow-up intervals [23, 24] . In patients with indeterminate pancreatic lesions or equivocal clinical findings, it could complement imaging and serum biomarkers by improving the noninvasive assessment of malignancy probability and disease aggressivenes s [6, 25] . In addition, this assay may aid preoperative risk assessment by identifying patients with biologically more aggressive PDAC who may benefit from intensified staging and neoadjuvant-oriented decision-making [26] .
The translational potential of these applications also depends on whether the underlying assay platform is sufficiently informative, practical, and scalable for clinical implementation. Compared with conventional cfDNA NGS and methylation-based assays, cfDNA 5hmC-Seal may offer several advantages that are particularly relevant to clinical usage . Conventional cfDNA NGS mainly captures genomic alterations or global fragmentomic patterns and may be limited by low tumor fraction, especially in early-stage disease [27] . By contrast, 5hmC-Seal enriches for a biologically informative subset of cfDNA molecules associated with active gene regulation and tissue-specific transcriptional programs, thereby potentially improving the detectability of disease-related epigenetic remodeling [12, 13] . An additional advantage of the present study is that both 5hmC coverage-based and fragmentomic features can be extracted from the same sequencing library, allowing simultaneous inte gration of two complementary layers of information without requiring separate assays. From a practical perspective, cfDNA 5hmC profiling can capture sample-level 5hmC characteristics at relatively low sequencing depth, without requiring the high-depth sequencing often needed for comprehensive genome-wide approaches. This feature could reduce cost and make the assay more suitable for large-scale clinical testing. Moreover, because 5hmC-Seal does not rely on harsh chemical conversion, it preserves DNA integrity and is more compatible with low-input plasma cfDNA. This feature may represent an important advantage over methylation-based assays that depend on conversion-based workflows, such as cfDNA whole-genome bisulfite sequencing (WGBS) , EM-seq, or TAPS , particularly when limited sample input and fragment preservation are critical [28] . Although direct head-to-head comparisons will still be necessary, these biological and technical characteristics support cfDNA 5hmC-Seal as a promising and clinically practical platform for noninvasive PDAC detection and biological stratification.
A major finding of this work is that discriminatory 5hmC alterations in PDAC were not confined to a single feature modality. Instead, gene-level, window-based, peak-based, OCR-associated, repeat-associated, fragment size, and end-motif features all showed disease-related changes, indicating that PDAC is accompanied by broad epigenetic remodeling in circulating cfDNA. This observation is generally consistent with many earlier cfDNA 5hmC studies [29, 30] and i t is also in line with cfDNA methylome studies suggesting that integrating epigenetic and fragmentomic information can improve the biological resolution of liquid biopsy [10] . At the same time, our findings extend prior work in two important ways. First, rather than supporting a model dominated by pancreas-derived tumor signal alone, the widespread perturbations observed here, together with tissue deconvolution results, suggest contributions from tumor-derived signals, host responses, and microenvironment-related remodeling across multiple tissues. Second, although we also observed strong fragmentomic differences, some of the 5hmC-associated end-motif and fragment-size patterns in our study were not entirely concordant with those reported in conventional WGS-based cfDNA fragmentomics , highlighting that 5hmC-enriched libraries may capture a biologically filtered subset of cfDNA rather than the full circulating DNA pool [31, 32] . Our findings therefore extend the conventional view of cfDNA biomarkers by showing that informative PDAC-associated signals may arise from a broader disease-associated epigenetic landscape, while also emphasizing that 5hmC-derived multi-dimensional features are not simply interchangeable with standard genome-wide cfDNA features.
Another important observation is that PDAC progression was accompanied by coordinated shifts in functional 5hmC programs. Stage-associated analyses suggested a transition from earlier developmental and metabolic programs toward later immune- and stromal-associated programs. These findings fit well with the current understanding that PDAC progression is not merely a matter of increasing tumor burden, but also involves progressive reprogramming of tumor-stromal interactions, inflammatory signaling, and extracellular matrix remodeling [17] . Notably, among the feature types evaluated in the staging model, OCR-related features carried the strongest predictive signal, suggesting that 5hmC changes associated with regulatory accessibility may be especially sensitive to progression-related biological changes , potentially because such features better capture shifts in active regulatory states across tumor compartments . At the same time, the overall staging performance remained limited. This is not entirely unexpected and is in line with previous cfDNA-based studies in which stage prediction has generally been more difficult than cancer-versus-control discrimination, likely owing to the biological continuity between adjacent stages and the relatively subtle molecular differences separating them [33] . Our results therefore support the presence of progression-related cfDNA 5hmC remodeling in PDAC, while also underscoring that translating these signals into robust stage classification remains challenging.
Our subtype analysis provides an additional layer of insight into PDAC heterogeneity. Most previous studies of cfDNA 5hmC in PDAC and other cancers have focused primarily on cancer-versus-control discrimination, with relatively limited attention to whether circulating epigenetic signals can resolve clinically relevant pathological subgroups [29, 30] . By defining pathological subtypes according to lymph node metastasis and vascular invasion status, we observed a stepwise progression pattern from the double-negative state to the dual-positive state, with the single-positive states occupying intermediate positions along the inferred trajectory . This framework is conceptually consistent with current understanding that lymphatic and vascular invasion represent related but non-identical dimensions of PDAC aggressiveness and may arise through partly distinct biological mechanisms [14, 15] . O ur results provide a further advance by suggesting that LM- and VI-associated routes may converge on a common terminal aggressive phenotype while retaining distinct molecular features and functional enrichments. Features associated with the VI-related route were more strongly linked to membrane-proximal signaling and regulatory responses, whereas LM-related features were enriched for developmental reprogramming, plasticity, and migratory or invasive remodeling . Although these route-specific programs still require external validation and mechanistic studies , our findings suggest that cfDNA 5hmC profiling may provide biologically meaningful subtype information beyond conventional binary classification, and therefore offer a potential noninvasive approach for dissecting aggressive PDAC phenotypes that are not captured by detection models alone.
From a modeling perspective, our study also provides a methodological advance over many previous cfDNA epigenetic studies by establishing a unified and task-adaptive analytical framework rather than relying on a single conventional classification pipeline. Recent cfDNA studies have increasingly recognized the value of multimodal integration for cancer detection [10, 34] , but many published models still emphasize endpoint-specific feature sets or relatively fixed machine-learning workflows. In contrast, our framework integrates supervised feature selection, task-specific feature transformation, and automated machine learning within a single modeling strategy. For example, subtype inference used a dedicated FNN framework with a custom objective function designed to jointly preserve pseudotime ordering and state discrimination. This design allowed the modeling strategy to adapt to the biological structure of each task rather than forcing all clinical questions into the same prediction architecture. In addition, because model evaluation can be unstable in relatively small cohorts, all levels of our framework were assessed using nested cross-validation, which provides a more robust estimate of generalization performance while reducing the risk of optimistic bias during model selection and hyperparameter tuning. Taken together, these features make our framework not only an integrated multi-feature model, but also a comparatively robust and extensible analytical system that can accommodate both standard clinical endpoints and more complex progression-related tasks.
Taken together, these findings support the potential of multi-dimensional cfDNA 5hmC profiling for PDAC detection and biological stratification. Nevertheless, several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, this was a single-center study and the non-PDAC cohort was only about one-third the size of the PDAC cohort. This design does not fully reflect real-world PDAC detection settings, where non-cancer controls greatly outnumber PDAC cases, and therefore the detection performance should be interpreted cautiously. However, the relatively large PDAC cohort also provided an advantage by enabling more comprehensive characterization of PDAC-associated heterogeneity across stages and pathological subtypes. Second, the staging model showed only moderate performance, suggesting that although cfDNA 5hmC contains progression-related information, adjacent stages remain difficult to distinguish robustly with the current feature space and cohort size. Third, the subtyping analysis should be considered preliminary and requires validation in independent cohorts, ideally with longitudinal follow-up. Although the inferred progression pattern from s00 to s01/s10 and finally to s11 was biologically interpretable and consistent with the observed pseudotime structure, it was reconstructed from cross-sectional clinicopathological states rather than directly observed over time. Therefore, the proposed LM- and VI-associated routes should be interpreted as a model of subtype-related heterogeneity rather than definitive evidence of temporal progression . Fourth, the fragmentomic features analyzed here were limited to 5hmC-enriched cfDNA and therefore do not fully represent the genome-wide fragmentation landscape captured by standard WGS-based cfDNA. Future studies should directly compare fragmentomic features derived from 5hmC-Seal and matched cfDNA WGS data to clarify how 5hmC-captured fragments differ from the total cfDNA fragment pool. Finally, future studies may further improve the clinical utility of this framework by integrating additional biomarker modalities, such as proteomics, radiomics, transcriptomics, and other molecular or clinical features. In parallel, mechanistic studies linking circulating 5hmC programs to tumor biology, stromal remodeling, and immune interactions may further clarify why particular feature classes perform better for specific tasks and may help identify new biological markers of PDAC aggressiveness.
M ethods
Study p opulation
A total of 274 participants were prospectively enrolled between January 2024 and December 2024 at Peking Union Medical College Hospital. The cohort comprised three groups: (1) a study group including 204 adult patients (≥18 years) with PDAC who underwent surgical resection or chemotherapy; (2) a benign disease group including 52 adult patients (≥18 years) with pathologically confirmed benign pancreatic tumor who underwent pancreatic tumor resection; and (3) a healthy control group consisting of 18 adult individuals (≥18 years) without tumor (non-neoplastic conditions) recruited from the hospital’s health examination center. Exclusion criteria were as follows: for the study group, individuals younger than 18 years or those without a pathological diagnosis of malignant pancreatic tumors were excluded; for the benign disease group, individuals younger than 18 years or those with uncertain or potentially malignant pathological diagnoses were excluded; for the healthy control group, individuals with any pancreatic tumor s or other digestive system malignancies, as well as those younger than 18 years, were excluded. This study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Peking Union Medical College Hospital (approval number: I-23PJ1544). Written informed consent was obtained from each participant.
S ample collection , cfDNA 5hmC profiling and sequencing
A pproximately 2~ 5mL of plasma was collected from peripheral blood samples after centrifugation at 1 600× g for 10 min at 4 °C and 16 000× g at 10 min at 4 °C. cfDNA was extracted using the Circulating Nucleic Acid Kit (Qiagen). 5hmC library construction was performed as previously described [12] . Pair-end 150 bp sequencing was performed on the Illumina Novaseq 6000 platform.
Data preprocessing
Raw paired-end reads were processed using fastp for adapter trimming and quality filtering, and reads shorter than 50 bp after trimming were removed. Clean reads were aligned to the hg38 reference genome using bwa mem . PCR duplicates were removed using sambamba markdup . Duplicate-removed BAM files were further filtered to retain only properly paired reads with mapping quality of at least 30 (MAPQ >= 30) using samtools view. For downstream paired-read analyses, primary properly paired alignments were extracted after excluding unmapped, secondary, and supplementary reads ( samtools view -h -b -f 3 -F 2316 ). Alignment statistics were collected at each major preprocessing step using samtools flagstat (Supplementary Table 2 ) .
Coverage and fragmentomic based features generated
Gene- base d 5hmC features were generated by quantifying reads mapped to gene-body regions from the filtered BAM files. Read counting was performed using featureCounts with a custom gene-body annotation file (hg38_genebody.gtf) . Paired-end reads were counted at the fragment level (-p -- countReadPairs ). After counting, genes with high sparsity were removed by excluding features with zero counts in at least 90% of samples. The remaining gene-level features were further filtered using variance thresholding ( VarianceThreshold , threshold = 0.01), and counts from duplicated gene names were collapsed by summation. The resulting filtered gene-by-sample matrix was used as the gene-body 5hmC feature set for downstream analyses.
Window-based 5hmC features were generated by dividing the reference genome into fixed-width windows (3k bp) using bedtools makewindows . Candidate windows were then filtered to retain only autosomal and chrX regions and to remove windows overlapping blacklist regions ( https://raw.githubusercontent.com/Boyle-Lab/Blacklist/master/lists/hg38-blacklist.v2.bed.gz ) . Additional filtering was performed based on window length, mean mappability , and GC content. Mean mappability for each window was calculated from a genome-wide m [4] appability bedGraph file using overlap-weighted averaging, and GC content was calculated using bedtools nuc . Only windows meeting the predefined mappability and GC thresholds were retained. For each sample, 5hmC fragment counts within the filtered windows were quantified using bedtools coverage -counts based on the fragment BED files. Window count files from all samples were then merged into a single window-by-sample count matrix. To reduce sparsity and low-information features, windows with zero counts in at least 90% of samples were removed, followed by variance filtering using VarianceThreshold with a threshold of 0.01. The resulting matrix was used as the final fixed-window 5hmC feature set for downstream analyses.
Sample-specific 5hmC-enriched peaks were first identified from fragment BED files using MACS2 callpeak (-g hs -f BED -- nolambda -- nomodel -- extsize 167) . For each sample, called peaks were filtered by signal-per-million (SPM) and fold-enrichment thresholds, restricted to chromosomes chr1–chr22 and chrX , and peaks overlapping blacklist regions were removed. Peak summits were then expanded to fixed 501-bp intervals centered on the summit (summit ± 250 bp), and overlapping peaks within each sample were resolved by retaining the peak with the higher SPM value. To generate group-level consensus peaks, filtered peaks within each group were merged using multi-intersection analysis. Peaks present in more than a predefined proportion of samples within a group were retained as consensus peaks. Consensus peaks from the two groups were then combined and merged to produce a unified consensus peak set. For each sample, fragment counts within the merged consensus peaks were quantified using bedtools coverage -counts, and peak counts from all samples were merged into a single matrix. Finally, peaks with zero counts in at least 90% of samples were removed, followed by variance filtering using VarianceThreshold with a threshold of 0.01. The resulting matrix was used as the consensus peak-based 5hmC feature set for downstream analyses.
OCR- based 5hmC features were generated using a predefined pancreatic cancer–related open chromatin region set [35] . OCR coordinates were first lifted over to the hg38 genome assembly using liftOver with a minimum match threshold of 0.95, and regions overlapping blacklist intervals were removed using bedtools intersect -v. For each sample, 5hmC fragment counts within the filtered OCR regions were quantified using bedtools coverage based on the fragment BED files, and sample-level counts were merged into a unified OCR-by-sample matrix.
To obtain the final OCR feature set, only regions on chromosomes chr1–chr22 and chrX were retained, and duplicated OCR intervals were removed. OCRs with zero counts in at least 90% of samples were excluded, followed by variance filtering using VarianceThreshold with a threshold of 0.01. The resulting filtered matrix was used as the OCR-associated 5hmC feature set for downstream analyses.
FSD features were derived from paired-end fragment BED files generated from primary properly paired alignments. To define genomic bins for FSD analysis, the reference genome was partitioned into fixed-width bins ( 3 M bp) , and bins overlapping blacklist regions were removed. GC content and overlap-weighted mean mappability were then calculated for each bin, and only bins passing the predefined GC and mappability thresholds were retained. FSD features were generated by quantifying fragment counts across genomic bins within predefined fragment length intervals. Fragment lengths were grouped into one broad short-fragment bin (20–100 bp), a series of intermediate bins spanning 100–220 bp with a fixed 10 bp bin width, and one broad long-fragment bin (220–800 bp). For each sample and each fragment-length interval, fragments within the corresponding size range were selected and counted across all filtered genomic bins using bedtools coverage. Counts from all length intervals and genomic bins were concatenated to generate the final FSD feature matrix .
End-motif features were generated from the filtered BAM files by calculating the frequencies of all possible 5′ end 4-mer motifs [18] . For each properly paired aligned read, the 4-bp sequence at the 5′ end of the aligned reference segment was extracted from the reference-mapped sequence using pysam . For reads mapped to the forward strand, the first four aligned reference bases were used; for reads mapped to the reverse strand, the last four aligned reference bases were reverse-complemented to represent the 5′ end motif in a strand-consistent manner. Motif counts were aggregated across all properly paired reads in each sample and normalized by the total number of counted reads to generate a 256-feature motif frequency profile per sample. In addition, a motif diversity score (MDS) was calculated for each sample based on the normalized Shannon entropy of the 4-mer frequency distribution. The resulting 4-mer frequency matrix and sample-level MDS values were used for downstream analyses.
Repeat-associated 5hmC features were derived using the standard ARTEMIS pipeline [36] , and the resulting feature matrix (artemis.csv) was imported for downstream analysis. This matrix contained sample-level signals across a broad set of repetitive-element annotations, including LINE, SINE, satellite, LTR, and other repeat classes/subfamilies. Before downstream analyses, the ARTEMIS matrix was preprocessed by setting the sample identifier as the row index and removing samples and repeat features with excessive sparsity. Specifically, samples and features with zero values in more than 80% of entries were excluded. For differential and modeling analyses, the retained ARTEMIS features were further transformed using log1p, and the resulting sample-by-feature matrix was used for downstream analyses.
Differential analysis and functional enrichment
Differential analysis between PDAC and non-PDAC was performed separately for each feature type using a repeated subsampling strategy to reduce the impact of class imbalance. In each analysis, all non-PDAC samples were retained, whereas PDAC samples were partitioned by 3-fold repeated splitting with 3 repeats, generating 9 datasets in total. Differential testing was conducted independently in each dataset, and features detected consistently across all 9 datasets were retained as robust PDAC-associated features. Co verage -based features, including gene-body, window, consensus peak, OCR, and FSD features, were first filtered to remove low-abundance features (total count sum >10) and were then analyzed using PyDESeq2 [37] . ARTEMIS features were filtered for high sparsity and transformed using log1p, followed by differential analysis with limma [38] . End-motif features, represented as proportions, were analyzed using beta regression with a logit link. For all feature types, multiple testing correction was performed using the Benjamini-Hochberg method, and significant features were defined using feature-type-specific q-value and effect-size thresholds. Features reproducibly significant in all 9 repeated analyses and showing consistent effect direction were used for downstream analyses. Functional enrichment analysis was performed separately for differential features type . For region -based features, including differential peaks, windows, and OCRs, BED regions were analyzed using rGREAT with all GO_BP terms [39] . Significant terms were defined as those with both adjusted binomial and hypergeometric P values <0.05. For gene features, GO over-representation analysis was performed using clusterProfiler [40] , and terms with adjusted P value <0.05 and q value <0.05 were retained. Tissue-specific enrichment analysis was performed using deTS by default parameters [41] .
Stage-related differential analyses were performed within the PDAC cohort only. Samples without stage annotation were excluded, and stage III and IV cases were merged into a single III/IV category. For each feature type, the corresponding sample-by-feature matrix was extracted and aligned to the annotated PDAC samples. To identify stage-informative features, each feature was first standardized across samples, and its association with stage was evaluated using mutual information ( mutual_info_classif ). Empirical P values were estimated by permutation testing (n = 500) with Benjamini-Hochberg correction. Features passing the predefined P-value threshold were retained for downstream program decomposition. The selected feature matrix was column-wise normalized by its mean and decomposed using non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) with Kullback -Leibler loss. This yielded a sample-by-component matrix (W) and a component-by-feature matrix (H), representing stage-associated latent programs and their feature contributions, respectively. The W matrix was subsequently standardized for visualization and compared across stage groups to characterize stage-related progression patterns. Program functional enrichment analysis was based on the integrated H matrix. Specifically, top-loading features from selected NMF components in the H matrices were integrated across feature modalities. Gene-based components were subjected to GO over-representation analysis using clusterProfiler , whereas region-based components were analyzed using rGREAT for ALL GO terms. Enriched terms passing the predefined significance threshold (P/q-adjusted < 0.05) were retained and merged into an integrated annotation table for cross-program comparison.
T issue of origin analysis
Tissue-of-origin analysis was performed using a reference atlas of tissue-specific 5hmC peak sets [13] . Reference narrowPeak files were parsed by tissue and replicate, and the total covered base pairs of each peak set were calculated. All reference peak intervals were then merged into a unified BED file while retaining peak-set identity. For each sample, 5hmC fragment coverage across the merged reference peaks was quantified using bedtools coverage, and a replicate-level tissue score was calculated as fragment count divided by the total base pairs of the corresponding reference peak set, scaled by 10^3. Replicate-level scores were averaged within each tissue to generate a sample-by-tissue score matrix, which was further normalized within each sample to obtain relative tissue contribution percentages.
P seudotime construction and trajectory feature s inference
S ubtyping analysis was performed using datatype-specific input matrices generated in the differential analysis step. For count-based feature classes, including gene, FSD, peak, window, and OCR, the input matrix was obtained by variance-stabilizing transformation of the corresponding raw count matrix using PyDESeq2. F or end motif and ARTEMIS, 4-mer proportion matrix and log1p transformed matrix were used as input. All input matrices were organized in tab-delimited sample-by-feature format, with rows representing samples and columns representing features. Sample states were defined according to lymph node metastasis (LM) and vascular invasion (VI) status as S00 (double negative) , S10 (just LM) , S01 (just VI) , and S11 (double positive) . After matching samples with valid state annotation, batch effects were corrected using limma :: removeBatchEffect , yielding a full batch-corrected matrix. Pseudotime was inferred from the filtered matrix using diffusion pseudotime (DPT) implemented in Scanpy [42] . M odel construction and hyperparameter optimization
For PDAC detection, models were trained separately for each feature modality using datatype-specific input matri x generated in the preprocessing and differential-analysis steps. For count-based feature classes, including gene, FSD, peak, window, and OCR, the input matrix was obtained by variance-stabilizing transformation of the corresponding raw count matrix using PyDESeq2. F or end motif and ARTEMIS, 4-mer proportion matrix and log1p transformed matrix were used as input. Hyperparameter optimization was performed within each outer training set using Optuna , t o address class imbalance, SMOTE oversampling was applied within the training pipeline. Feature selection was also modality specific: artemis features were directly filtered by HSICLasso , motif features were filtered by mutual information ranking, and the remaining feature classes were first reduced by mutual information filtering and then refined by HSICLasso . The number of selected features was optimized during inner-loop tuning, and the best-performing pipeline in each outer fold was retained for evaluation.
For stage prediction, models were trained on the same datatype-specific matrices as mentioned before . Stage III and IV samples were merged into a single III/IV category, and stage labels were encoded as a multiclass outcome. Within each outer training set, Optuna was used to optimize both the number of PCA components and the feature-selection strategy. HSICLasso was applied when the number of input features exceeded the predefined threshold; otherwise, the original feature set was retained. The optimized pipeline therefore consisted of feature selection, PCA, and classifier fitting, and performance was summarized across outer folds.
For subtype modeling, regression-based progression models were constructed using the subtyping matrices and sample-level pseudotime values inferred from the trajectory analysis. Only four feature modalities (gene, peak, window, and OCR) were used. Samples were collapsed into three ordered states (S00 = 0, S10/S01 = 1, and S11 = 2) for ranking-based supervision, while continuous pseudotime values served as regression targets. Within each outer training set, features were first reduced to the top 4,000 variables ranked by mutual information with the three-level stage label . Optuna optimized the number of HSICLasso-selected features, network architecture, learning rate, and early stopping rounds. The prediction model was a fully connected neural network trained with a composite loss combining Pearson correlation loss and logistic ranking loss, with equal weights assigned to the two components. The ranking term enforced the order S00 < S10/S01 < S11, enabling the learned progression score to jointly capture continuous pseudotime structure and ordinal subtype progression.
